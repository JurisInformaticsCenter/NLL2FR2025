International Workshop on Methodologies for Translating Legal Norms into Formal Representations (LN2FR 2025)

9 December 2025, University of Turin, Italy

Associated with JURIX 2025

Call for Contributions

The aim of this workshop is to promote concrete progress toward machine-readable law, with the potential to impact fields such as autonomous systems, legal tech, digital governance, and compliance automation. The workshop will provide a stimulating environment for exchanging ideas and building interdisciplinary bridges among legal scholars, researchers, practitioners, and tool developers.

We particularly welcome submissions that report practical implementation experiences, such as the formalization and operational use of legal rules and regulations, or the development of tools, frameworks, or datasets for legal norm formalization. We also plan to gauge interest in a future shared task or benchmark dataset, and invite contributions of aligned corpora, annotation tools, or other relevant resources. Topics of interest include but are not limited to, the following:

  • Knowledge representation methods applicable to legal norms, including different types of (deontic) logic or comparable formalisms
  • Formalization of rule-exceptions, rule-conflicts and/or contrary-to-duty obligations
  • Formalization of abstract legal concepts and basic principles of law
  • Models and approaches to the practical implementation of law-formalisations (e.g.,(legal) ontologies, LegalRuleML, PROLEG, Logical English, reasoning engines, SAT-solvers)
  • Tools and frameworks supporting (semi-)automated legal formalization
  • Technical and legal challenges in formalizing legal texts for computational applications
  • Case studies and implementation reports
  • Reusable outputs, including tools, logic patterns, or shared datasets
  • Verification and validation of formal legal representations in practice
  • Hybrid approaches, such as combining symbolic logic with machine learning
  • Controlled Natural Languages (CNLs) as tools for legal formalization
  • Argumentation theory and formal models of legal interpretation
  • Natural Language Processing (NLP) for Legal Rule Extraction and Formalization
  • Large Language Model (LLM)-assisted translation of legal texts into formal representations

Submission Details

Contributions may be regular/long papers (up to 10 pages) or short papers (up to 5 pages), excluding references. All papers must be original and not simultaneously submitted to another journal, conference, or workshop. Each submission will be reviewed by at least two program committee members.
